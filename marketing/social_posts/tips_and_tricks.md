## Post 5: Salary Negotiation Basics

**LinkedIn:**
How to negotiate salary without losing the offer.

Most candidates make these mistakes:
- Accept the first offer (leave money on the table)
- Give a number first (lose negotiating power)
- Focus only on base salary (miss total compensation)

Here's the smart approach:

**Step 1: Delay Salary Talk**
When asked "What are your salary expectations?" early in the process:

"I'd love to learn more about the full scope of the role first. I'm sure we can find a number that works for both of us once we've established mutual interest."

**Step 2: Let Them Go First**
If pressed:
"What's the budgeted range for this position?"

or

"Based on the research I've done, I'm seeing ranges from $X to $Y for similar roles in this market. Does that align with what you have budgeted?"

**Step 3: Negotiate the Full Package**
Base salary is just one component. Also negotiate:
- Signing bonus
- Annual bonus/equity
- PTO days
- Remote work flexibility
- Professional development budget
- Earlier performance review

**Step 4: The Counter Offer**
When you receive an offer:

"Thank you for the offer! I'm excited about the opportunity. Based on my experience with [specific skills] and the market rate for [role] in [location], I was expecting something closer to $[15-20% higher]. Can we work together to get closer to that number?"

**Step 5: Get It in Writing**
Once agreed, get everything documented before giving notice at your current role.

**Real Example:**

Original offer: $85K
After negotiation: $95K + $10K signing bonus + 2 extra PTO days
Effective first-year value: $107K

Negotiation took 3 emails over 2 days.

## Post 6: Red Flags to Watch For

**LinkedIn:**
10 Job Posting Red Flags You Shouldn't Ignore üö©

After reviewing millions of job postings, here are the warning signs of potentially problematic employers:

**1. "We're like a family"**
Translation: Poor work-life boundaries, expect 24/7 availability
Look for: Clear discussion of work-life balance instead

**2. "Wear many hats"**
Translation: Understaffed, you'll do 3 people's jobs for 1 salary
Look for: Clear role definition and responsibilities

**3. "Must thrive in fast-paced environment"**
Translation: Chaos, poor planning, constant emergencies
Look for: "Well-organized" or "structured" environment

**4. "Competitive salary"**
Translation: Below market rate
Look for: Actual salary range listed

**5. "Entry-level, 5+ years experience required"**
Translation: Confused employer or unrealistic expectations
Look for: Realistic requirements matching level

**6. "Seeking rockstar/ninja/guru"**
Translation: Immature culture, likely tech bro environment
Look for: Professional terminology

**7. "Unlimited PTO"**
Translation: Pressure not to take time off, no payout when leaving
Look for: Specific PTO allotment (15, 20, 25 days)

**8. "Great opportunity for exposure"**
Translation: Low/no pay
Look for: Concrete compensation details

**9. Job posted for 90+ days**
Translation: Unrealistic requirements, lowball salary, or ghost job
Look for: Recently posted positions

**10. "Must be passionate about [company product]"**
Translation: Expect you to work for passion, not fair compensation
Look for: Focus on professional skills and growth

**How to Verify:**

‚úì Check Glassdoor reviews
‚úì Look up the hiring manager on LinkedIn
‚úì Ask specific questions in the interview

## Post 8: Cover Letter Mistakes

**LinkedIn:**
5 Cover Letter Mistakes That Kill Your Chances

After analyzing thousands of cover letters, here's what NOT to do:

**Mistake #1: Starting with "Dear Sir/Madam"**
Shows you didn't research who's hiring.

‚úÖ Fix: Find the hiring manager's name (LinkedIn, company site, or call reception)
If impossible: "Dear [Department] Team" or "Dear Hiring Manager"

**Mistake #2: Repeating Your Resume**
"As you can see from my resume, I worked at..."

Your cover letter should ADD to your resume, not repeat it.

‚úÖ Fix: Explain the story behind your achievements
"When I joined Company X, retention was at 60%. I knew we needed to..."

**Mistake #3: Focusing on What You Want**
"This role would be a great opportunity for my career growth..."

They don't care about your growth. They care about their problems.

‚úÖ Fix: Focus on what you'll contribute
"I noticed your recent Series B focuses on enterprise expansion. In my last role, I led the enterprise sales strategy that increased revenue 200%..."

**Mistake #4: Being Too Long**
1000-word essays that recruiters won't read.

‚úÖ Fix: 3 short paragraphs
- Why you're excited about THIS company (specific)
- Your most relevant achievement for THIS role
- Clear next step

**Mistake #5: Generic Templates**
"I am writing to express my interest in the position..."

Boring. Forgettable. Shows no effort.

‚úÖ Fix: Start with a hook
"When I saw that Acme Corp is launching a new product line in sustainable packaging, I immediately thought of my experience..."

**The 3-Paragraph Framework:**

**Paragraph 1: The Hook**
Why THIS company excites you (be specific)

**Paragraph 2: The Proof**
Your most relevant achievement with numbers

**Paragraph 3: The Close**
What you'd specifically contribute + call to action

**Example:**

"Your recent blog post on scaling engineering teams resonated with me. I faced a similar challenge at StartupCo when we grew from 10 to 50 engineers in 8 months.

I implemented a structured onboarding program that reduced ramp-up time by 40% and created a mentorship framework that improved retention by 25%. I'd love to bring similar systematic approaches to Acme's scaling challenges.

I'd welcome the opportunity to discuss how my experience in hyper-growth environments could support your team's expansion. I'm available for a call this week at your convenience."

## Post 10: Networking Strategy

**LinkedIn:**
How to network without feeling sleazy.

Most people think networking is:
- Asking strangers for jobs
- Attending awkward events
- Pretending to care about golf

Real networking is:
- Building genuine relationships
- Providing value first
- Playing the long game

**The Give-First Framework:**

**Step 1: Identify Connection Opportunities**
Target people who are:
- In roles you want (future you)
- At companies you admire
- Solving problems you understand

**Step 2: Research Before Reaching Out**
- Read their recent posts/articles
- Find common interests/connections
- Understand their current challenges

**Step 3: Lead with Value**
Don't ask for a job. Offer something:
- "Saw your post about X, here's a tool that helped me..."
- "I noticed your company is expanding to Y market, I have experience there..."
- "Read your article on Z, thought you might appreciate this resource..."

**Step 4: Make It Easy**
Bad: "Can we hop on a call sometime?"
Good: "Would you be open to a 15-minute virtual coffee next Tuesday at 2pm?"

**Step 5: Follow Through**
- Show up prepared
- Respect their time (end early if possible)
- Send a thank-you note with a specific takeaway
- Stay in touch (comment on their posts, share relevant articles)

**The Coffee Chat Framework:**

:00-:03 - Warm up, thank them
:03-:08 - Ask about their career journey
:08-:12 - Ask specific questions about their role/industry
:12-:14 - Share your situation briefly
:14-:15 - Ask if they know anyone else you should talk to

**Never ask for a job directly. Ask for:**
- Advice on your career path
- Insights about the industry
- Feedback on your approach
- Introductions to others

**The Follow-Up Sequence:**

Day 1: Thank you email with specific appreciation
Week 2: Share article relevant to your conversation
Month 1: Update them on advice you implemented
Month 3: Offer to make an introduction for them
Month 6: Check in with a genuine question

**Where to Network (Online):**

LinkedIn:
- Comment thoughtfully on posts (not "Great post!")
- Share insights from your experience
- Join industry-specific groups

Twitter:
- Engage in industry conversations
- Share your learnings and projects
- Build in public

Industry Slack/Discord:
- Answer others' questions
- Share resources
- Participate in discussions

**Where to Network (In-Person):**

- Industry conferences (worth the investment)
- Meetups in your field
- Professional association events
- Alumni gatherings
- Volunteer for causes you care about

**The Long Game:**

Good networking takes months/years. You're building:
- Relationships, not transactions
- Reputation, not just a job
- A career network for decades
